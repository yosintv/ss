The History of Spider Solitaire — From 1949 to Windows to the Web

Updated May 17, 2026 · 7-minute read

Spider Solitaire is everywhere — on every Windows PC for a generation, on every smartphone’s app store, and in every browser. But where did Spider Solitaire come from? Who invented Spider Solitaire? And how did this two-deck patience game become one of the most-played card games in the world? This Spider Solitaire history walks from the game’s mid-20th-century origins to its breakout via Microsoft Windows and into the modern era of online Spider Solitaire.

The Origins of Spider Solitaire: Patience Books in the 1940s

The earliest documented references to Spider Solitaire — or close variations like “Tarantula” — appear in patience and Solitaire compendia from the late 1940s. The most widely cited source for the game’s introduction is the 1949 book Culbertson’s Card Games Complete by Albert H. Morehead and Geoffrey Mott-Smith, which presented Spider as a difficult two-deck patience game played across ten columns.

Spider Solitaire historians sometimes point to Franklin D. Roosevelt as one of the game’s notable early enthusiasts, though that attribution is folklore as often as fact. What is well-documented: Spider Solitaire was a niche enthusiast’s game for decades, played mostly by patience-game hobbyists with two physical decks of cards.

The Pre-Computer Era: Spider Solitaire on the Kitchen Table

Before computers, Spider Solitaire was slow. Setting up the tableau alone — 54 cards across ten columns, only the bottom face-up — took a minute. Dealing the stock required manually flipping ten cards onto ten columns, often disturbing carefully arranged runs. A single Spider Solitaire game commonly took 30–45 minutes of real-world play.

That friction kept the audience small. Spider Solitaire’s reputation in the pre-computer era was as an “intellectual” patience — harder than Klondike, more demanding than FreeCell, and frequently unwinnable, which scared off casual players.

1998: Microsoft Spider Solitaire Arrives on Windows

The moment that transformed Spider Solitaire forever was its 1998 inclusion in the Microsoft Plus! 98 pack for Windows 98 — a paid add-on. The game then ships free with Windows Me in 2000 and with every subsequent consumer release of Windows. Suddenly, every Windows PC owner has a copy of Microsoft Spider Solitaire one Start-menu click away.

Microsoft Spider Solitaire introduces three difficulty modes — 1 Suit, 2 Suits, and 4 Suits — that became the canonical Spider Solitaire variants almost overnight. Before Microsoft, Spider was usually played in one form (the four-suit version). After Microsoft, “Spider Solitaire” meant “the game with the difficulty slider” to most of the world.

The Windows Years: Cultural Phenomenon

Between 2000 and roughly 2015, Microsoft Spider Solitaire became one of the most-played games on Earth — not because it was a great game (it was), but because it was the default game on hundreds of millions of office computers. Spider Solitaire became shorthand for “a productive way to procrastinate at work,” and the game’s green felt background became one of the most recognizable backgrounds in computing.

By the mid-2000s, win-rate statistics — which Microsoft Spider Solitaire tracked persistently — had become a quiet status symbol among office workers. A 70% Spider Solitaire 4 Suits win rate was a flex. A 5-minute Spider Solitaire 1 Suit completion was a flex.

The Mobile and Web Era: Spider Solitaire Goes Online

When Microsoft removed pre-installed Spider Solitaire from Windows 10 in 2015 and replaced it with the ad-supported “Microsoft Solitaire Collection,” an opening appeared. Open-source and indie web developers built free, no-login Spider Solitaire alternatives, and the game found a second life online.

Modern online Spider Solitaire games — including spidersolitaire.xyz — typically include features the original Microsoft Spider Solitaire never had: daily Spider Solitaire challenges with shared seeds, XP-based progression, achievements, unlockable card backs and themes, and god-mode power-up panels for relaxed play. The game’s strategic core hasn’t changed since 1949, but the experience around it has.

Spider Solitaire Variants Over Time

Beyond the canonical 1 Suit, 2 Suits, and 4 Suits modes, Spider Solitaire has spawned dozens of variants:

  • Spiderette — a single-deck version played across seven columns. Faster, smaller, but loses much of the strategic depth.
  • Will-O’-the-Wisp — also single-deck, slightly different setup, often grouped with Spiderette as a “starter Spider”.
  • Scorpion — relative of Spider with different stock-deal rules. Often offered as “Scorpio Mode” in modern Spider Solitaire clients.
  • Three-deck Spider — a brutal three-deck version with twelve foundations. Mostly an academic curiosity.

Spider Solitaire Today

In 2026, Spider Solitaire is more accessible than ever. Free online Spider Solitaire games run in every browser, on every phone, with progress that syncs and persists. Modern features like daily Spider Solitaire challenges and XP progression have rejuvenated the game for a new generation while preserving the 75-year-old patience-game core.

The game’s 1949 designers would recognize today’s Spider Solitaire instantly. The rules are unchanged. What’s different is everything around them — the speed of setup, the unforgiving stock deal that’s now a tap, the persistent stats that turn each Spider Solitaire game into a data point on a long-running curve.
